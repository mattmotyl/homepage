    Chapter 2 · What Data Do Platforms Collect?

    Platforms collect a large amount of personal data, network data, advertising information, and engagement signals from their users. Some information is volunteered, some is inferred, and some is purchased from third parties. Not every platform gathers the same fields, but together these categories describe the universe of variables researchers may encounter when requesting Article 40 access.

    The examples above come directly from the EDMO report. Platforms can usually produce many more variables inside each category, especially when they combine behavioural logs with predictive models.

    Why these data matter for research

    Platforms rely on these signals to power machine learning systems, advertising optimisation, integrity enforcement, and product experimentation. Understanding which pieces exist—and where they originate—helps you request the right tables and interpret transparency disclosures. For example:

    • User-provided and behavioural data reveal what happened on the platform and who was involved.
    • Signals received from other users and device telemetry capture community-level reactions and potential abuse.
    • Inferences and purchased datasets often drive ranking, safety, or revenue decisions yet rarely appear in public APIs.

    When you ask a platform to fulfil an Article 40 request, reference these categories explicitly. It shows that you understand the internal landscape and reduces ambiguity about the granularity you expect.

    Trusted access programs

    These programs act as the front door for deeper, non-public datasets. Review their cadence and scope to align your project timelines and make sure your Article 40 requests reference the appropriate channel.

    Staying current without official changelogs

    Join transparency communities

    EDMO task forces, the Integrity Institute, and All Tech Is Human convene practitioners who share early intel on schema shifts and newly released datasets.

    Document every delivery

    Keep a copy of the data dictionaries and tables you receive. Treat them as version-controlled artefacts.

    Log open questions

    When you notice gaps or ambiguous fields, note them in your research journal so you can follow up with the platform or triangulate with other datasets later.
